## About This Sample

This document shows editorial QA work applied to three grammar-topic sections from a commercially published K–6 EFL teacher's guide. The problems were identified during actual classroom use. Each section is reviewed with QA annotations anchored to exact source text, followed by a plain-language rewrite.

What this proves: editorial QA judgment on instructional documentation, plain language editing under a defined reader profile (teachers reading mid-class, under time pressure), cognitive load awareness, and before/after reasoning visible at the sentence level.

The source curriculum is a real commercial series. It has been anonymized for portfolio use as **Project Acorn: Bilingual Early Education Series**.

---

## QA Issues Identified

These problems recur across all three sections. They are the basis for every annotation below.

| # | Issue | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Steps buried in prose paragraphs — no numbering | Teacher cannot scan for next action; loses place mid-delivery |
| 2 | Grammar explanation and teaching procedure written in the same sentence | Teacher cannot tell whether a sentence is instruction or background knowledge |
| 3 | Ambiguous pronouns with no clear referent | Teacher has to infer who or what "they" refers to before acting |
| 4 | No outcome statement per section | Teacher has no signal for when the phase is done or what students should be able to say |
| 5 | Complex syntax inappropriate for mid-class reading | Embedded clauses and long parenthetical asides slow real-time processing |
| 6 | Undefined references ("the icon," "the highlighted expression") | Teacher must already know what these refer to — new or substitute teachers cannot follow |

## Topic A — Subjunctive Mood: Demand / Suggest / Insist

### BEFORE (anonymized source text)

> With Activation Step 1 complete (Intermediate level), write a "1st" over the first subjects at the start of each sentence, circle the verbs *demand, suggest, insist, recommended*, and underline the subjects *it, he, he and she* in each of the examples. First, ask students what kind of meaning those sentences carry — routine or something stronger. Second, ask them whether the highlighted verbs are expressing routine or habits (which would be expressed by simple present tense) until you have them tell you that these verbs appear not in the simple present but in their base form (bare infinitive). Then, open the grammar rule panel and give them your quick explanation on the use of the subjunctive.

---

### QA Annotations — Topic A

**QA note — Issue type: Steps buried in prose**

> `"write a "1st" over the first subjects... circle the verbs... and underline the subjects... First, ask students... Second, ask them... until you have them tell you... Then, open the grammar rule panel"`

Five discrete actions are written as one continuous sentence. A teacher reading this mid-class has to parse the sequence while facing students. There are no numbered steps, no visual break between actions. The risk is that a step gets skipped or the teacher has to re-read to recover the sequence.

---

**QA note — Issue type: Instruction mixed with grammar explanation**

> `"(which would be expressed by simple present tense)"`

This parenthetical explains *why* the simple present is relevant — it is background knowledge for the teacher, not a teaching instruction. Embedded inside a directive sentence, it interrupts the action chain. The teacher cannot tell whether this is something to say aloud to students or something to understand privately before acting.

---

**QA note — Issue type: No outcome statement**

The section ends with "give them your quick explanation on the use of the subjunctive" — with no signal for what students should be able to produce or say by the time the section closes. "Quick explanation" tells the teacher nothing about scope, depth, or the stopping point.

---

### AFTER — Topic A

**Goal:** Students identify that demand-type verbs (demand, suggest, insist, recommend) are followed by the base form of a second verb, not the simple present. They name this pattern as the subjunctive.

**Activation Step 1** must be completed before beginning this section (Intermediate-level students).

**Step 1.** On the board or projected sentences, write **1st** above the subject of each sentence.

**Step 2.** Circle the verbs: *demand, suggest, insist, recommended*.

**Step 3.** Underline the second subject in each example: *it, he, he and she*.

**Step 4.** Ask: *"What do these sentences express — a routine, or a desire or demand?"*
Allow students to answer. Confirm: desire or demand.

**Step 5.** Ask: *"Are these circled verbs in the simple present tense?"*
Guide students until they identify that the verbs after the subject are not in the simple present — they are in the base form (bare infinitive).

**Step 6.** Open the grammar rule panel.
Give a brief explanation of the subjunctive using the rule shown.

**Stopping point:** Students can identify that demand-type verbs are followed by the base form of a second verb and can label this as the subjunctive.

## Topic B — 2nd Conditional + Wish / If Only / As If / As Though

### BEFORE (anonymized source text)

> Starting with Activation Step 1 complete (Intermediate level), circle all the subjects before "were", "found" and "had occurred" and have them tell you they're all past forms of verbs. From there, have students consider what might bring a past form into these contexts while you point out to the highlighted expression "if, wish, if only, as if" present in all the examples (which is the reason the verbs following it are in simple past or past perfect). Before moving to the rule panel, remind students that inversion is an option in 2nd conditional sentences — covered in a previous lesson. Open the grammar rule panel and quickly explain this use of subjunctive.

---

### QA Annotations — Topic B

**QA note — Issue type: Ambiguous pronoun**

> `"circle all the subjects before "were", "found" and "had occurred" and have them tell you they're all past forms of verbs"`

"They're all past forms" — *they* is ambiguous. It could refer to the circled subjects or to the verbs *were, found, had occurred*. The teacher needs to tell students that these verb forms are past — but the instruction does not name which words students should be looking at when they say this. A substitute teacher or new hire reading this cold cannot resolve it without already knowing the activity.

---

**QA note — Issue type: Instruction mixed with grammar explanation**

> `"while you point out to the highlighted expression "if, wish, if only, as if" present in all the examples (which is the reason the verbs following it are in simple past or past perfect)"`

"Which is the reason the verbs following it are in simple past or past perfect" is an explanation of the grammar rule — not an instruction. It appears parenthetically inside a multi-action sentence. The teacher should know this before the activity begins, not while executing two concurrent actions (asking a question and pointing to a visual simultaneously).

---

**QA note — Issue type: Undefined reference**

> `"the highlighted expression"`

Which highlighting? The source does not specify whether this refers to pre-printed highlighting in the student book, a projected slide, or something the teacher has just marked on the board. A teacher using a different display format cannot follow this instruction.

---

**QA note — Issue type: No outcome statement**

> `"quickly explain this use of subjunctive"`

No scope or stopping point. The section covers inversion, four trigger expressions, and the past/past perfect distinction — a range that a "quick explanation" does not delimit.

---

### AFTER — Topic B

**Goal:** Students identify that *if, wish, if only, as if,* and *as though* trigger past-form verbs (simple past or past perfect), and that 2nd conditional sentences with *to be* can use inversion for emphasis.

**Activation Step 1** must be completed before beginning this section (Intermediate-level students).

**Step 1.** In the example sentences, circle the verbs *were*, *found*, and *had occurred*.

**Step 2.** Ask: *"Are these verbs in the past tense?"*
Students confirm: yes.

**Step 3.** Ask: *"Why do you think these sentences use the past tense?"*
While students consider this, direct their attention to the expressions *if, wish, if only, as if* — these appear in each example sentence.

> **Teacher note:** These expressions are the reason the following verb takes the simple past or past perfect form. This is the subjunctive at work. Students do not need to name this rule yet — just observe the pattern.

**Step 4.** Ask students to state the connection: *"These expressions are followed by..."*
They should complete: a past-form verb.

**Step 5.** Remind students that 2nd conditional sentences using *to be* can use inversion for emphasis. (Students covered inversion in the previous unit — this is a recall prompt, not new content.)

**Step 6.** Open the grammar rule panel.
Give a brief explanation covering: the four trigger expressions, past/past perfect form, and inversion with 2nd conditionals.

**Stopping point:** Students can identify the trigger expressions, state that they require a past-form verb, and recall inversion as an option in 2nd conditional sentences with *to be*.

## Topic C — Reported Speech

### BEFORE (anonymized source text)

> Once Activation Steps 1 and 2 are done (Intermediate-level students), look at the right column of the examples and underline or circle every instance of *he said*, *she said*, *he told*, *she asked*, then ask students if the person who is saying these sentences is the same person who said the ones in the left column (after they say, yes, you can also ask if they both express the same idea – which is also a yes). Ask them if the tense used in the reported speech column is the same as the one in the quoted speech column. After this, you should ask them to tell what tense is being used in the first column and what tense is being used in the second column for each of the lines in the chart of their books. Open the grammar rule panel and quickly give them your explanation of the rules. Make sure to cover, at this point, the interrogative examples — ask what word appears in the reported speech version that was not in the original. Students should identify *if* or *whether*. After that, look at the sentences with WH- words and note that the WH- word is carried into the reported version. Similarly, show students how an imperative is expressed in reported speech. Also cover how a negative order is reported — these examples are in their books and visible on screen, with no audio.

---

### QA Annotations — Topic C

**QA note — Issue type: Steps buried in prose — most severe instance**

This is the longest single-paragraph instruction across all three topics. It contains at least nine distinct teacher actions: mark reporting verbs, ask about speaker identity, ask about shared meaning, ask about tense, ask about tense per row, open rule panel, address interrogatives, address WH- words, address imperatives and negative orders. None are numbered. Two are flagged as "at this point" or "after that" — relative sequencing language that requires the teacher to track position mentally while also managing the class.

The practical risk: a teacher mid-delivery has no quick re-entry point if interrupted. "At this point" and "after that" are not recoverable anchors.

---

**QA note — Issue type: Ambiguous pronoun**

> `"ask students if the person who is saying these sentences is the same person who said the ones in the left column (after they say, yes, you can also ask if they both express the same idea)"`

"They both" — does this refer to the two columns, the two persons, or the two sentences? The parenthetical intended to say "yes, both columns express the same idea" — but "they" without a noun anchor could be parsed as "the two speakers." The teacher must interpret this before responding to students.

---

**QA note — Issue type: Complex syntax inappropriate for mid-class reading**

> `"Ask them if the tense used in the reported speech column is the same as the one in the quoted speech column. After this, you should ask them to tell what tense is being used in the first column and what tense is being used in the second column for each of the lines in the chart of their books."`

These two sentences accomplish consecutive related tasks — compare the tenses, then name the tense per row — but the second sentence is a single clause with two embedded "what tense is being used in" constructions. A teacher reading this for the first time while standing in front of a class has to parse parallel embedded clauses before knowing what to ask.

---

**QA note — Issue type: No outcome statement**

Nine instructional actions with no stated goal. The teacher has no way to know whether "quickly give them your explanation" means two minutes or ten, or what students should be able to produce when this section ends.

---

### AFTER — Topic C

**Goal:** Students recognize that reported speech uses reporting verbs (said, told, asked) and shifts verb tenses back from the original. They identify how questions, WH- questions, imperatives, and negative orders are reported.

**Activation Steps 1 and 2** must be completed before beginning this section (Intermediate-level students).

**Step 1.** In the right column of the chart, underline or circle all instances of *he said / she said / he told / she asked*.

**Step 2.** Ask: *"Is the person speaking in the right column the same person who spoke in the left column?"*
Students confirm: yes.

**Step 3.** Ask: *"Do both columns express the same idea?"*
Students confirm: yes.

**Step 4.** Ask: *"Is the verb tense in the right column the same as in the left column?"*
Students identify: no — the tense shifts.

**Step 5.** Go through the chart row by row. For each pair, ask students to name:
- The tense used in the left (quoted speech) column
- The tense used in the right (reported speech) column

**Step 6.** Open the grammar rule panel. Give a brief explanation of reported speech and the tense-shift pattern.

**Step 7.** Direct students to the interrogative examples. Ask: *"What word was added to the reported speech version?"*
Students identify: *if* or *whether*.

**Step 8.** In the WH- question examples, point out that the WH- word has been carried over into the reported version.

**Step 9.** Direct students to the imperative examples. Show how a command is reported using *told + infinitive*. Then show how a negative order is reported.

> **Note:** The imperative and negative-order examples are in the student book and on-screen. There is no audio for these.

**Stopping point:** Students can describe the tense-shift rule, identify how *if/whether* functions in reported questions, and recognize how imperatives and negative orders are reported.

## Skills Demonstrated

- Identifying documentation QA problems by type — not by general impression — and anchoring each finding to the exact text that causes the risk
- Distinguishing between two categories that prose instructions routinely collapse: teacher background knowledge (what to understand) and classroom action (what to do)
- Rewriting for a specific, pressured reader — a teacher mid-class who needs to scan, not parse
- Preserving all grammar content exactly: the rules, the example sentences, the tense names, and the teaching sequence are unchanged; only the instruction delivery is improved
- Applying numbered steps as a QA intervention — not a stylistic preference — because unordered prose creates re-entry failure when a teacher is interrupted
- Adding outcome statements as a structural requirement: the teacher needs a stopping condition, not just a list of actions
